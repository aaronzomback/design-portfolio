TimeSink is an on-going personal project. I came up with the name one day when I was daydreaming and connected the image of a dripping faucet with the collection of time that we experience in films. Currently, I am writing code for an MVP web app using Ruby on Rails. In this case study, I present how I would create the mobile app.

Establishing a new platform for filmmakers...

For many filmmakers promoting their film and trying to find audiences can be difficult and overwhelming.

For creators, the film industry can seem daunting. With no direct pathway towards finding an audience, independent filmmakers are encouraged to become self-reliant on distribution which often results in creating rugged individualism and tireless self-promotion.

As media becomes easier to produce and consume, it paradoxically becomes more difficult for audiences to find and appreciate independent film because there's just so much content out there.

I got the idea of creating a new platform called TimeSink that would help filmmakers connect with audiences by transforming the experience of art-house cinemas into an online network.

What I learned from my research

Investigating conversations between anonymous filmmakers

"There has to be a place where the cream can rise and right now I don't see a lot of opportunities for that to happen anymore."
"In the old days, the only way you could get your movie in front of an audience was to enter it into a film festival. But now you can put your work online and promote it yourself."
"They cynically use our submission fees to generate revenue, and in many cases, don't even bother to watch the films."
CONTEXT IS KEY

As we see in art museums, art-house cinemas, and other galleries, the environment and context that art is experienced in plays a significant role in how the artwork is perceived. Creating a dedicated space for films to redirect filmmakers away from a highly saturated site like YouTube, will help focus audience expectations and shine a valuable spotlight on both the films and its creators.

Instead of more opportunities for self-monetization or self-distribution, filmmakers need more support and a healthy atmosphere where their work can flourish. This way they don't have to be casted on the same level as cheap entertainment.

This is where I think TimeSink can become successful. By leveraging the flaws in the current film festival model, this type of a film platform is not in competition with TikTok, Netflix, or YouTube, but instead its mission will be to develop honesty, transparency, and respect in a niche marketplace to support the present and future generations of filmmakers.
