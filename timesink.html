The platform for
filmmakers

For creators, the film industry can seem daunting. With no direct pathway towards finding an audience, independent filmmakers are encouraged to become self-reliant on distribution.

I got the idea of creating a new platform called TimeSink that would help filmmakers connect with audiences by improving the experience of film festivals and art-house cinemas into an online network.

As we see in art museums, art-house cinemas, and other galleries, the space that the work is experienced strongly influences how it is perceived. TimeSink will help focus audience expectations away from the quick bites of entertainment that we see on massive sites like YouTube, and shine a valuable spotlight on the films and its creators.

For many filmmakers promoting their film and trying to find audiences can be difficult and overwhelming.

Filmmaker pain points

  • Most film festivals aren't able to provide the value that filmmakers expect: inspired audiences, distribution deals, & industry connections.

  • Many filmmakers are becoming disillusioned by the lack of transparency from festivals.

  • i.e. Festivals often charge submission fees without providing proof of viewing submitted films.

  • The sheer quantity of content makes it difficult to stand out and connect with audiences.

With no confirmation that their film submission was viewed, filmmakers often feel that they're wasting their money on festival submission fees.

Filmmakers need more support and a healthy atmosphere where their work can flourish.

Transforming the arthouse
experience into an online network

AFA is an arthouse theater in New York City, founded by Jonas Mekas in the 1970s.

While living in New York City, I was abe to experience the arthouse theater, Anthology Film Archives. Often it would show retrospectives, but sometimes it would spotlight work by current filmmakers. Such a place is able to promote an encouraging atmosphere for the art form; free of any box office expectations. What if there was a way to improve this theater-going experience by transforming it into an online network featuring more current filmmakers and making it easier to socialize with other cinephiles. Transforming this experience into an online network would create a more accessible and interactive film culture. Its presence could inspire a new generation of filmmakers.

Context is key

TimeSink will create a better platform for filmmakers by focusing on cinematic traditions, artistic vision, honesty, respect, and curation rather than popularity.

It's not in competition with massive video sharing sites like Youtube, Vimeo, TikTok, and Instagram. The appeal is not towards the mega-studio superhero film, but a niche audience of both creators and arthouse theater-goers.

It will compete with film festivals by improving the film submission process, the presentation, and the audience participation.

TimeSink is more similar to film festivals and arthouse theaters,
rather than massive video hosting apps

Improving the submission
experience

Wireframing the submission process.

I wanted to have a status bar that'd update the filmmaker on the status of their submission.

I also wanted to provide the written critique to each rejected filmmaker so they can learn from the decision.

A filmmaker can also view their submission history.

Submission Status

A filmmaker can access their submission status, which includes 4 successive and automatically updated timestamps presented through a status bar. Each timestamp provides the confirmation of each action to the filmmaker.

  1. Received
  2. Viewed
  3. Critiqued
  4. Decision

After the decision has been made the written critique will be posted to the filmmakers' submission status page.

Submission History

For memory and organization the filmmaker can also view their submission history:

  • Film title
  • Date submitted
  • Date of decision
  • Decision (In progress/Selected/Not Selected)

Quick Submission Flow

The checkout flow will keep the filmmaker informed throughout the process with proper affordance cues. They will be allowed to review their order to make sure all of their details are correct before submitting.

The payment system will be flexible being able to accept a wide variety of payment methods. It's important that TimeSink accepts bitcoin since it is globally adopted, it can help attract a larger audience.

Scaling the process

As TimeSink receives more submissions it will be important to vet and select a team of curators who bring refined taste, a knowledge of cinema history, as well as an appreciation for the art form. In order to sustain high-quality critiques for each rejected filmmaker, the distribution of work must be optimized between each juror. Once we find the best juror/film ratio, we will be able to match the scale to fit any volume of film submissions. To sustain the integrity of the process, communicating the criteria for acceptance on TimeSink will be extremely important. An efficient backend program must also be created to handle the automated status updates on each submission.

Moving forward

It will be great to develop partnerships with other creative institutions such as arthouse cinemas, filmmaking workshops, film programs, and other artist collectives. All of these will help expand the scope and the audience for TimeSink. Eventually TimeSink can gain revenue from sponsorships. Creating content to expand the platform's outreach through Twitter, Instagram, and YouTube, as well as maintaining a consistent brand personality, will be essential to the development of the platform.
