Before We Begin

If I were to claim that my design process was sparkling clean I'd be full of dust. In fact it's a clashing of ideas: reverse movement, forward movement, a circling back. I guess they call it iteration. Anyways, TimeSink is an on-going personal project of mine. I came up with the name one day when I was daydreaming and connected the image of a dripping faucet with the collection of time that we experience in films. Currently, I am writing code for an MVP web app using Ruby on Rails. In this case study, I present how I would create the mobile app. Thanks for reading and if anything I hope that I don't bore you.

Prologue

As a filmmaker I consider the film industry to be like the wild west. There is no direct pathway towards growing an audience, securing distribution, or attracting investments. This is especially true for those who have little to no connections.

We can try for our own distribution and upload films to YouTube or Vimeo but I've considered those platforms to be a far from ideal home for filmmakers; being oversaturated and too generalized. With few exceptions, most films hosted on such platforms will simply get lost among the noise of endless content. Film festivals can be helpful, but there still seems to be too many incoherencies. Couldn't there be something else that offers a more impactful and sustained culture which could be like the vanguard of contemporary and independent film?

I believe that there needs to be a centralized catalyst in the film industry that not only helps emerging filmmakers break through the noise and get the support they need to continue their art form, but also a curated film community where people can expect to discuss, meet, and discover.

Research

Before thinking about a design for a new film platform, I first wanted to conduct research in order to validate problems shared by many filmmakers.

Instead of interviewing other filmmakers, I chose to investigate online forums - like reddit and nofilmschool.com - for discussion between anonymous filmmakers. I thought that this type of a sales safari method would be more efficient in diagnosing current filmmakers’ pain points as opposed to conducting personal interviews.

I began to notice a consensus emerging - filmmakers’ were disappointed that film festivals provided little value and support towards developing their careers.

I discovered that most filmmakers submit to and attend film festivals in order to find collaborators, gain representation, secure distribution, or attract investments.

PROCESS INSIGHT I don’t want to get lost in the wrong feedback loop based on my limited assumptions. It was necessary for me to separate my own experiences and emotions in order validate what other filmmakers’ pain points might be.

INVESTIGATING CONVERSATIONS BETWEEN FILMMAKERS ON MESSAGE BOARDS

The Problem

There doesn’t seem to be a trusted and viable place for emerging independent film talent to establish their work. Aside from a handful of larger film festivals, many filmmakers no longer trust nor consider them to be relevant, especially with personal representation and distribution options available such as YouTube and Vimeo. This distrust appears to be caused by a lack of engagement and a lack of value generated towards their careers. Additionally, festivals often charge submission fees without providing proof of viewership.

These impersonal experiences cause filmmakers to feel marginalized, disillusioned, and discouraged.

Who are we designing for?

To best inform the overall product design - from how it looks and feels, to how it works, I first had to identify exactly who this platform will be designed for.

If we want to differentiate TimeSink from platforms such as YouTube or Vimeo, it’s not enough to design for filmmakers and viewers, but more so the design must be crafted towards a specific persona and idea.

At this stage I identified TimeSink’s niche user; namely those who appreciate the art and history of cinema. By doing this, we not only begin to articulate the types of films that TimeSink would curate (those who appreciate history often have developed taste) but also the brand personality, design principles, and overall theme. Already, this is different than a massive online hosted video platform like YouTube. It is about becoming more specialized and I think by doing so, it raises the possibility of creating a super-loyal user base.

FINDING OUR NICHE BY IDENTIFYING A TARGET AUDIENCE

Vision

While focusing on the present, we can also begin to establish new traditions and reward artistry that could inspire future generations of filmmakers. So beyond becoming a reputable film platform that attracts capital, we want TimeSink to become a cultural phenomenon that serves as a beacon of creative light for current and future generations of filmmakers.

To catalyze the process of creation and discovery, TimeSink must be able to attract both viewers and filmmakers into the same network. To make this convergence possible, we must design a service that provides value to each, and I believe that the most significant shared value can be distilled into one magnanimous idea; growth.

To the filmmaker value is growth in audience, ideas, collaborators, recognition, and investors. We want filmmakers to be honored and excited about the possibility of having their films be presented on the platform. We want them to trust in the idea that a selection to TimeSink most likely means valuable support and exposure. To the viewer, value is growing a sense of belonging to a healthy community and a sense of participation in the creative act. It is growth in discovering new films, connecting with other minds, having great discussions, and perhaps making new friends.

Furthermore, the goal for TimeSink is to become both a PAINKILLER - by encouraging filmmakers through honesty, support, and trust, and a VITAMIN - by creating a vibrant network where users feel a sense of belonging and are motivated to keep returning back to.

In order to create a robust community, it is necessary to create one that is accessible at all times. It can’t be limited like a festival typically is to only a couple of days per year. So what features can help achieve all of the above?

PROCESS INSIGHT | My role as a product designer is to understand and empathize with the users, but I must also be able to integrate their feedback to discover solutions beyond what they see.

Building a mobile app

Deciding to build TimeSink as a mobile app was an obvious choice for me as it optimizes accessibility.

There are 8 basic models:

  1. User - user account system, login, sessions.
  2. Film - films presented in a gallery-style showcase.
  3. Film review - presented on each film’s show page, any user can write a film review.
  4. Film submission - users will be able to submit films to be considered for presentation on TimeSink.
  5. Blog - presenting articles written about cinema and opinion pieces about current events/politics. Stimulate debate and discussion. Comments section below each article.
  6. Forum - an open space where users can make posts about anything. Featuring comments below each post. This provides another opportunity for users to mingle and connect.
  7. Comment - Users can comment beneath each film review, blog post, and forum post, and they can also comment on other comments (recursive).
  8. Reputation - Users can earn reputation by receiving upvotes so they can grow to distinguish themselves within the community and earn other rewards.

Features

User onboarding, it's personal.

TimeSink immediately welcomes the new user by personalizing the sign-up process. The user will decide their own title by identifying themselves as either a cinephile, a filmmaker, a critic, or an investor.
