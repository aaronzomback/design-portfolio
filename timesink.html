The platform for
filmmakers

For creators, the film industry can seem daunting. With no direct pathway towards finding an audience, independent filmmakers are encouraged to become self-reliant on distribution.

I got the idea of creating a new platform called TimeSink that would help filmmakers connect with audiences by improving the experience of film festivals and art-house cinemas into an online network.

As we see in art museums, art-house cinemas, and other galleries, the space that the work is experienced strongly influences how it is perceived. TimeSink will help focus audience expectations away from the quick bites of entertainment that we see on massive sites like YouTube, and shine a valuable spotlight on the films and its creators.

For many filmmakers promoting their film and trying to find audiences can be difficult and overwhelming.

The amount of content on Youtube keeps growing. It's difficult for filmmakers to stand out. Source: https://pex.com/blog/state-of-the-youtube-address/

Filmmaker pain points

  • Most film festivals aren't able to provide the value that filmmakers expect: large audiences, distribution deals, & industry connections.
  • Many filmmakers are becoming disillusioned with the lack of transparency from festivals.
  • i.e. Festivals often charge submission fees without providing proof of viewing the film
  • Some filmmakes think that there is simply too much content these days, which makes it difficult to stand out.
  • At the best festivals, filmmakers often have to compete with other "indie" films that have budgets over >$100,000, which makes it difficult to compete.

Filmmakers need more support and a healthy atmosphere where their work can flourish.

CONTEXT IS KEY

As we see in art museums, art-house cinemas, and other galleries, the environment and context that art is experienced in plays a significant role in how the artwork is perceived. Creating a dedicated space for films to redirect filmmakers away from massive sites like YouTube, and will help focus audience expectations and shine a valuable spotlight on both the films and its creators.

Instead of more opportunities for self-monetization or self-distribution, filmmakers need more support and a healthy atmosphere where their work can flourish. This way they don't have to be casted on the same level as cheap entertainment.

This is where I think TimeSink can become successful. By leveraging the flaws in the current film festival model, this type of a film platform is not in competition with TikTok, Netflix, or YouTube, but instead its mission will be to develop honesty, transparency, and respect in a niche marketplace to support the present and future generations of filmmakers.
